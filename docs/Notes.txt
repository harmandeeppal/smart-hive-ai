Breakdown of the Design Improvements
Here's why this new layout is more effective, based on your requirements:

a. Contextual Visuals (Not Just Gauges)
Your original radial gauges are clean but lack context. This design uses more informative visuals for each sensor.

Temperature & Humidity: These are represented by linear range bars. This is intuitively better for showing a value within a specific optimal range.

The bar is clearly divided into three zones: too low, optimal ([=====]), and too high.

The current value is marked with a # or is implicitly where the bar ends.

Crucially, the exact optimal values (e.g., 32-36°C) are written below the bar for clear reference.

Vibration: Vibration isn't about an "optimal" range but about detecting anomalies from a baseline.

The visual is a simple magnitude bar showing the current activity level from "Calm" to "Agitated."

A Trend sparkline (__./¯\_) is added. This is a powerful, compact way to show if the hive has been calm, recently disturbed, or is consistently agitated.

Sound: Sound is complex; a single dB value doesn't tell the whole story.

The design breaks it down into two key components: Volume (dB) and Frequency (Pitch). A queenless hive might not be louder, but its frequency is higher (a "roar"). This dual-bar approach provides a richer diagnostic.

b. Clear Status and Actionable Advice
This is the most critical improvement. Below each visual, there are two new lines:

Status: A plain-English summary of the current state. Instead of the user having to interpret "37.1 °C," the dashboard tells them it's "Too Hot."

Action: This is the decision-support feature. When a parameter is out of the optimal range, it provides a simple, direct recommendation.

Example: If the temperature is too high, it suggests: "Consider ventilation or shade."

Example: If the sound profile indicates a high-frequency roar, a future version could suggest: "Action: Schedule manual inspection for queen."

c. Intelligent Live Feed Card
The live feed card is no longer just a video box. It's an AI status panel.

The ASCII art shows a clear bounding box around a detected queen.

The AI Status line provides feedback on what the model is doing ("Actively Scanning," "QUEEN DETECTED").

It also confirms the last successful snapshot time, giving the user confidence that the cloud storage feature is working.

d. Simplicity is Maintained
Despite adding more information, the design adheres to your request for simplicity.

The one-row, card-based layout is preserved.

The typography and spacing are clean and uncluttered.

The primary numeric value is still the most prominent element in each card, allowing for a quick glance.

This design elevates the dashboard from a simple data display to a true beehive management tool, which is perfect for a Master's level project.

2. Relative Humidity (BME280 Sensor)
Humidity is crucial for preventing the eggs and larvae from drying out and for the critical process of turning nectar into honey.

Optimal Range: 50% to 70% Relative Humidity (RH)

Anomaly: Too High (≥75% RH) 💧

ALERT: HIGH HUMIDITY ALERT: Check for moisture and poor ventilation.

Actions:

Ensure all upper and lower vents are clear and open to allow moist air to exit.

Verify that the hive roof, bottom board, and inner cover are not trapping water or condensation.

If using solid covers, consider replacing or venting them to allow for better vapor exchange.

Anomaly: Too Low (≤45% RH) 💦

ALERT: LOW HUMIDITY ALERT: Consider supplemental water.

Actions:

Provide a clean, accessible external water source (with landing spots like pebbles) near the hive entrance.

If the hive is being fed, ensure the syrup isn't overly concentrated, which forces bees to forage for more water.

3. Vibration/Activity (LIS3DH Sensor - Accelerometer/RMS)
Vibration data (analyzed using Root Mean Square, or RMS) is an indicator of the colony's physical activity and state. Optimal levels are relative to the colony's typical behavior for that time of day.

Optimal Range: Consistent RMS level with the colony's historical baseline for the current time of day and season.

Anomaly: Vibration Agitated (RMS significantly higher than baseline) 🚨

ALERT: AGITATION ALERT: Hive disturbed or under attack.

Actions:

Check the visual feed for external pests (e.g., small animals, strong winds) or unusual activity near the hive.

Avoid approaching or opening the hive for at least two hours to allow the colony to calm down.

If high RMS persists, schedule a careful, late-day inspection to check for aggressive behavior or potential attack.

Anomaly: Vibration Low/Still (RMS significantly lower than baseline during active hours) 🛑

ALERT: LOW ACTIVITY ALERT: Possible cluster immobilization or weakness.

Actions:

Check the weather history for sudden drops in temperature or heavy rain which naturally stop activity.

If the weather is mild, schedule a light, non-invasive check for signs of weakness, queen loss (which reduces fanning activity), or potential pesticide exposure.

4. Acoustic Profile (Samson USB Microphone)
Acoustic monitoring detects specific sounds that indicate the colony's internal state, such as communication signals or distress.

Optimal Profile: Normal, low-frequency hum, typically peaking around 200−300 Hz.

Anomaly: Queenless Roar (High-Pitched/Distressed Sound) 👑

ALERT: QUEEN STATUS ALERT: Abnormal sound detected. Schedule inspection.

Actions:

Immediately check the brood frames for eggs, very young larvae (which confirm a queen is present), or the presence of emergency queen cells.

If the colony is confirmed queenless, introduce a new queen or a frame of young brood immediately.

Anomaly: Swarming Signals (Piping/Quacking) 🏃

ALERT: SWARM PREDICTION ALERT: Imminent swarming possible.

Actions:

If "Piping" (∼500 Hz) or "Quacking" (lower frequency) is detected during swarming season, the primary swarm is likely to leave very soon.

Schedule a colony split (dividing the colony) to relieve congestion and satisfy the bees' urge to swarm.

Anomaly: Sudden Silence (Dramatic drop in all acoustic energy during active hours) 💀

ALERT: DISTRESS/MORTALITY ALERT: Investigate external chemical cause.

Actions:

Check for local reports of pesticide use in the foraging area.

Conduct a visual inspection for dead or paralyzed bees at the entrance, which is a key sign of poisoning.
